Beginning Compressions

Cardiopulmonary resuscitation (CPR) is an emergency procedure used to help restore blood circulation and breathing in a person who is not breathing and has no pulse. The steps for CPR include the following:

  1. take standard precautions and ensure unresponsiveness
  2. place the person on a firm, flat surface
  3. Begin with 30 chest compressions at a depth of 2in(5cm) and a rate of 100-120 compressions per minute. The chest should completely recoil after each compression
  4. After 30 compressions, give 2 rescue breaths by tilting the person's head back, lifting their chin, and blowing into their mouth over 1 seconduntil you see the chest rise and resume compressions.
  5. If an AED is available, use it as soon as possible and follow instructions. If not proceed to next step
  6. Continue the cycle of 30 compressions and 2 breaths until emergency responders arrive or the person shows signs of life.

*NOTE*

If multiple rescuers are available, switch roles of giving rescue breayhs and compressions every 2 minutes or after 5 cycles of CPR to prevent fatigue.

If and AED is attached, analyzation should occure every two minutes.

Performing Chest Compressions

Step 1

Take standard precautions, place the heel of one hand over the center of the chest (lower half of the sternum).

Step 2

Place the heel of your other hand over the first hand.

Step 3

With your arms straight, lock your elbows, and position your shoulders directly over your hands to press the sternum at a rate of 100 to 120 compressions per minute and to a depth of 2 to 2.4 inches (5 to 6 centimeters), using a downward movement. Allow the chest to return to its normal position. Do not lean on the chest between compressions. Compression and relaxation should be of equal duration.

*NOTE*

Complications from chest compressions are uncommon but may include broken ribs, a fractured sternum, or injury to organs such as the liver. While these injuries cannot always be prevented, proper hand placement and correct technique can reduce the likelihood of them occurring. The possibility of injury should never delay starting CPR or providing high-quality compressions. Compressions should be smooth, steady, and continuous. Quick, forceful jabs are ineffective for generating artificial circulation. Keep the heel of your hand in contact with the patient’s chest during the relaxation phase, but fully release pressure on the sternum so the chest can return to its normal position between compressions.

Opening the Airway

An open airway is necessary for effective rescue breathing. Two common techniques used to open the airway in adults are the head-tilt chin-lift maneuver and the jaw-thrust maneuver. These techniques move the tongue forward and away from the throat so air can pass.

The head-tilt chin-lift is the most common method and is used when there is no suspicion of spinal injury. In some patients, this maneuver alone may restore normal breathing. If vomit or foreign material is present in the mouth, quickly remove visible material before attempting rescue breathing.

If a spinal injury is suspected, use the jaw-thrust maneuver to minimize movement of the neck. Place your fingers behind the lower jaw and lift upward to open the airway while keeping the head in a neutral position. If the mouth remains closed, use your thumbs to pull down the lower lip. If the jaw-thrust does not successfully open the airway, the head-tilt chin-lift should be used.

Maintaining an open airway is the highest priority when caring for a patient, especially in trauma situations, because it is essential for survival.
